Types of Biases

DeBiasMe analyzes text for a comprehensive range of bias types organized into a hierarchical classification system. Understanding these categories can help you identify and mitigate biases in your own communication.

1. Human Bias

Human biases arise from our cognitive processes, social dynamics, and individual experiences. These biases shape how we perceive and interpret information.

Individual Biases

Biases that operate at the personal level, influencing how we process information and make judgments.

  • Behavioral: Assumptions about how someone will act based on stereotypes or limited observations
  • Interpretation: Skewing the meaning of information based on preconceptions
  • Belief Persistence/Principle: Holding onto established beliefs despite contradictory evidence
  • Selective Adherence: Inconsistently applying principles to favor certain outcomes
  • Spotlight Effect: Overestimating how much others notice our actions or appearance
  • Situation Framing: Presenting scenarios in ways that emphasize certain aspects while downplaying others
  • Human Expertise: Over/undervaluing human judgment based on status or credentials
  • Presentation: Judging information based on how it's displayed rather than content
  • Ranking: Creating hierarchies even when distinctions are minimal or arbitrary

Group Biases

Biases that emerge from group dynamics and collective decision-making.

  • Groupthink: When desire for harmony leads to irrational decision-making
  • Bandwagon: Adopting beliefs because they're popular
  • Deployment: Favoring familiar approaches regardless of appropriateness
  • Sunk Cost Fallacy: Continuing endeavors due to previous investment despite poor outcomes

Cognitive Biases

Systematic patterns of deviation from norm or rationality in judgment.

  • Confirmation: Seeking information that confirms existing beliefs
  • Anchoring: Relying too heavily on the first piece of information encountered
  • Availability Heuristic: Judging likelihood based on how easily examples come to mind
  • Dunning-Kruger Effect: Overestimating abilities when knowledge is limited
  • Implicit Bias: Unconscious attitudes affecting understanding and decisions
  • Automation Complacency: Over-relying on automated systems

2. Statistical/Computational Bias

These biases emerge from how we collect, process, and interpret data, affecting the validity and reliability of our analyses.

Selection and Sampling

Biases that arise from how data is collected and samples are chosen.

  • Data Generation: When collection processes systematically favor certain outcomes
  • Detection: When methodology is more likely to find certain types of results
  • Collective Fallacy: Drawing conclusions about individuals based on group data or vice versa
  • Evaluation: When assessment methods advantage certain groups
  • Selection: When sample data isn't representative of the target population
  • Treatment: Systematic differences in how cases are handled
  • Assignment: Non-random allocation of subjects affecting outcomes
  • Popularity: When frequently occurring items receive disproportionate weight
  • Population: When study groups don't reflect target populations
  • Simpson's Paradox: When trends appear in groups but disappear when combined
  • Temporal: When timing-related factors systematically influence data
  • Uncertainty: Systematic errors in representing probabilities

Processing/Validation

Biases that occur during data analysis and interpretation.

  • Amplification: When processes magnify small differences in data
  • Inherited: When new systems adopt biases from predecessors
  • Error Propagation: When initial mistakes compound through analysis steps
  • Model Selection: Choosing frameworks that favor certain patterns
  • Regression: Systematic errors in statistical models for different groups

Use and Interpretation

Biases in how we apply and interpret analytical results.

  • Activity: Disproportionately representing users with higher engagement
  • Concept Drift: When meaning of data changes but models remain fixed
  • Energy: Favoring options with lower computational/cognitive costs
  • Feedback Loop: When outputs influence future inputs, creating self-reinforcing patterns
  • Data Bridging: Inappropriately transferring conclusions between different contexts

3. Systemic Bias

These biases are embedded within social, political, and institutional structures, shaping outcomes at a systemic level.

Historical

Inequities embedded in data and systems due to historical practices and policies.

Historical biases perpetuate past injustices in current systems, creating persistent advantages for historically privileged groups and barriers to equity despite current intentions.

Societal

Biases that reflect and reinforce existing social prejudices, stereotypes, or inequalities prevalent in broader culture.

Societal biases normalize harmful cultural stereotypes, create systems that mirror rather than correct social inequities, and establish barriers to equal participation for marginalized groups.

Institutional

Policies, practices, and procedures within organizations that systematically advantage certain groups while disadvantaging others.

Institutional biases create structural barriers to equity, lead to persistent disparities despite individual good intentions, and establish self-perpetuating systems of advantage and disadvantage.
